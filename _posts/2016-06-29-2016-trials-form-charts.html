ANALYSIS

It’s a young field with more questions than answers. The biggest question is whether anyone will represent the U.S. in Rio in the 20K, as no American racewalker has met the qualifying standard of 1:24:00. Though John Nunn and Trevor Barron have gone faster in the past, neither has done it during the 2015-16 qualifying period. Race walk aficionados are also wondering about Barron’s fitness and technique, as he has competed sparingly and without much success since the 2012 Games. The one vet in a generally young field is the favorite, Nunn, a 38-year-old U.S. Army Staff Sergeant. Nick Christie and Emmanuel Corvera are both promising but unproven young race walkers, and the 20-year-old Peters is yet another potential star on the rise.

ANALYSIS

Unlike some previous race walk trials, the U.S. is assured of having at least two competitors in Brazil this summer. Maria Michta-Coffey and Miranda Melville got their Olympic qualifiers at the Asian Race Walk Championships this spring and are head-and-shoulders above the rest of the field. This might be 2005 South Eugene grad Erin Gray’s last chance to make an Olympic team because she’s completing medical school and will begin her residency in the near future. Gray is a good bet for third place but must finish under the standard of 1:36:00 to book her ticket to Rio. Katie Burnett is Gray’s most likely challenger but also needs the standard. The rest of the field would need major improvement to contend for the top four, but the mix of vets and younger walkers should make it interesting.

ANALYSIS

Always a fan favorite at Hayward Field, the big men rarely fail to deliver quality entertainment, primarily to fans sitting in the West Grandstands. The favorite is Joe Kovacs, the 2015 world champion coached by Art Venegas. Kovacs led the world in 2015 and does so again in 2016. He’s coming into that sweet spot in his career that grizzled veterans such as Reese Hoffa, Christian Cantwell and Adam Nelson experienced some years ago. Now is the time for Kovacs to collect medals, titles and personal bests. On the first day of the Trials, he’s expected to leave such contenders as Hoffa, Ryan Crouser, Kurt Roberts and Jordan Clarke in his wake. Oregon native Crouser, from that celebrated family of throwers, should be well received at Hayward.

This event is top-heavy, with experienced jumpers Brittney Reese, Tianna Bartoletta and Janay DeLoach Soukup. Reese, 29, has been a fixture at the elite level for nearly a decade and has two Olympic Trials wins, an Olympic gold medal in 2012 and a 2016 World Indoor title on her résumé. DeLoach Soukup, 30, was the U.S. champion in 2013. Good luck cracking that triumvirate. Tori Polk and Funmi Jimoh, both 32, are also rich in experience. That hardly leaves room for a youngster like Alabama’s NCAA champ Quanesha Burks, 21, or Kentucky’s Kenyattia Hackworth, 22.
