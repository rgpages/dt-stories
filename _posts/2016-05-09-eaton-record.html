Former Duck’s decathlon performance was the highlight of the 2012 Olympic Trials

By Chris Hansen | The Register-Guard
May 9, 2016

A legend was born on June 23, 2012, the day Ashton Eaton survived the elements, captured the moment and broke the world record in front of his legions of fans.

There have been decades worth of memories created at Hayward Field, which is closing in on its 100th birthday, but few have been as uplifting, prideful or joyous as when Oregon’s favorite son — Portland born, central Oregon raised and schooled as a Duck — won the Olympic Trials decathlon and set the world record with a 9,039-point performance in the driving rain.

“He loves this place," his coach Harry Marra said afterward.

Eaton won seven of the 10 events over the two days of competition as he bettered the record of 9,026 held for 11 years by Roman Sebrle of the Czech Republic.

Eaton knew he was on record pace after clearing 17 feet, 4 1/2 inches in the pole vault.

With two events to go — the javelin and 1,500 meters — he asked Marra what it would take to get the American record.

“He stopped and looked at me, and said ‘Ashton, the world record,’" Eaton said that day. “At that moment, I saw his belief in me, and I said ‘Let’s go do it.’"

After throwing 193-1 in the javelin, Eaton had to run 4 minutes, 16.37 seconds in the 1,500 to get the record.

With the crowd of 21,795 on their feet, the gun went off and Eaton settled in behind Curtis Beach and Joe Detmer. They were at 1:07 after 400 and 2:18 after 800. When Eaton hit the final lap, he was two seconds off pace.

Then the crowd carried him home.

With the assembled mass urging him on, Eaton dug deep and finished the final lap in 62 seconds, with Beach and Detmer stepping aside with 10 meters to go, waving Eaton to the finish line.

He crossed in 4:14.48 and was soon met on the track by his mother, Roslyn, and future wife, Brianne Theisen-Eaton, who draped him in hugs.

“That was the coolest moment of my entire life, by far," Theisen-Eaton said. “I just cried, and he was pretty choked up and trying to catch his breath. I didn’t know how much he had left in the tank, but I knew he was going to give it his all."

Also waiting for Eaton at the finish line was a gathering of 20th century decathlon gold medalists: Dan O’Brien (1996), Caitlyn (Bruce) Jenner (1976), Bill Toomey (1968), Milt Campbell (1956), Rafer Johnson (1960) and Bill Thorpe, the son of the first Olympic gold medalist, Jim Thorpe (1912).
