Memorable moments from the 2016 U.S. Olympic Track & Field Trials

By Rob Denton and staff | The Register-Guard
July 11, 2016

Well, the Olympic Trials did not disappoint. Hayward Field hosted nine days filled with celebration and heartbreak.

Here are some of the highs and lows from the Trials. As always, there are a lot of great stories and photos in our Track & Field section.

Devon Allen
Men’s 110 hurdles

Devon Allen embraced the moment, holding up an “O" as he stood behind his starting blocks when introduced before the final of the 110-meter hurdles on July 9. Then he went out and ran a 13.03 to put himself on the team bound for Rio. He powered away from the field over the closing hurdles and won decisively. Ronnie Ash and Jeff Porter claimed the two other Olympic spots, both in 13.21 and separated by a thousandth of a second. Read more about the event here and the celebration here.

 
Women’s 200

On the last day of competition Oregon’s Deajah Stevens stunned the crowd, coming in second with a time of 22.30 was behind winner Tori Bowe, 22.25. Allyson Felix finished fourth in the 200 meters in 22.54, just a hundredth behind former Oregon sprinter Jenna Prandini, who tumbled across the finish line. Read more about the event here.

Bernard Lagat
Men’s 5,000

Bernard Lagat, the 41-year-old statesman of American distance running, is going to the Summer Games for the fifth time in his long career after a stunning victory in the men’s 5,000 meters July 9. Lagat closed with a 52.82-second final lap to steal the victory and secure his spot on Team USA in what was a spectacular four-man finish. Read more about the race here.

Alysia Montano
Women’s 800

The women’s 800-meter final was marred by a collision coming out of the final curve and form chart favorite Alysia Montano fell to the track. She eventually finished the race, nearly a minute after her fellow competitors, to cheers from the crowd. Kate Grace won the race in 1:59.10 in one of the more shocking victories of these Olympics Trials. The 25-year-old Yale graduate was injured last season. Read more about the race here and about Montano here.

Brenda Martinez
Women’s 1,500

Brenda Martinez edged Amanda Eccleston by .03 seconds with a dive at the line to place third in the women’s 1,500 meters. The dramatic finish resounded even more with Martinez after she was a favorite in the 800 on Monday before getting caught up in a collision with 120 meters to go that ended her Olympic dream in that event. There wasn’t much drama at the front of the race. Jenny Simpson pulled ahead on the third lap and won in 4:04.74, and Shannon Rowbury pulled away from the rest of the field during the final 100 to finish second in 4:05.39. Read more about the event here.

English Gardner
Women’s 100

Six Ducks made the women’s 100-meter semifinals, three made the final, one is an Olympian. Former Oregon sprinter English Gardner is headed to Rio in August, earning her first spot in the Summer Games with a blazing performance on July 3. Read more about the race here and about Gardner here.

Sydney McLaughlin
Women’s 400 hurdles

New Jersey high schooler Sydney McLaughlin, 16, was third in 54.15 ­setting a world junior, American junior and national high school record on the last day of the event. Ashley Spencer was a surprising second in 54.02. Spencer is the first woman ever to make finals at the U.S. trials in both the flat 400 and 400 hurdles. She barely made it out of the first round as the 16th qualifier and ran the final out of lane eight. Dalilah Muhammad won the women’s 400-meter hurdles in 52.88, by far the fastest time in the world this year. Previous fastest was 53.51 by Texas A&M’s Shamier Little, who did not make the finals. Read more about the event here.

Galen Rupp
Men’s 10,000

Galen Rupp’s eighth career 10,000 victory secured his chance to double at the Summer Games in Rio and made him the first American to win eight straight titles in any event on July 1. Venerable Bernard Lagat, 2015 World Outdoor Championships qualifier Hassan Mead, former Duck Eric Jenkins and 2016 NCAA runner-up Futsum Zienasellassie all did not finish the race. Read more here.

Allyson Felix
Women’s 400

Allyson Felix moved from fifth to first in the final 100 meters to finish in 49.68 seconds, the fastest time in the world since she ran 49.26 to win the World Championships last year. It was the third-fastest time of her career under adversity that had her coach proclaiming it Felix’s best moment. Former Oregon star Phyllis Francis was second with a personal best of 49.94, and Natasha Hastings grabbed the final Olympic spot in 50.17. Read more here.

 
Women’s high jump

Heading to Rio in the women’s high jump are a three-time Olympian and mother of three, a teenage world champion and daughter of an NFL quarterback, and an athlete returning from a doping suspension, festooned with more than 30 tattoos and wearing a two-piece red outfit topped off by a blue Mohawk haircut. They are, respectively, 32-year-old Chaunté Lowe, 18-year-old Vashti Cunningham and 29-year-old Inika McPherson. Read more here.

Ashton Eaton
Men’s decathlon

Ashton Eaton didn’t set a world record this weekend, but he did send a message. The Olympic champ, competing at less than 100 percent physically, is still considerably better than everybody else. He won his fourth U.S. title on July 3, scoring 8,750 points to qualify for his second Summer Games. Read more about this year’s performance here and about his record-setting 2012 performance here.
